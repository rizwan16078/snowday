🧠 PHASE 1 — check my tone below


 Tone Profile Document: The "Academic Survival Guide"
Core Identity The tone for this blog acts as the "Empathetic Nerd." It is the smart, older sibling or experienced classmate who has survived the exact same academic panic the reader is currently experiencing. The voice is deeply relatable, mathematically precise, and entirely focused on de-escaalating the reader's anxiety with cold, hard facts and a touch of humor.
Tone Reassuring, conversational, analytical, and slightly self-aware. It treats the stakes of a test score with the seriousness the student feels, but grounds the solution in simple, undeniable logic.
Writing Voice Primarily second-person ("You") and first-person plural ("We"). It builds immediate camaraderie by acknowledging the universal dread of grading systems, then seamlessly transitions into an authoritative guide on how to calculate and interpret those grades.
Sentence Structure Patterns
* The Emotional Reset: Uses very short, punchy sentences or fragments to validate emotion or deliver a hard truth.
* The Logical Breakdown: Uses longer, clearly sequenced sentences to explain the math so the reader does not have to expend mental energy figuring it out.
Vocabulary Patterns Blends institutional academic terminology (GPA, syllabus, weighted average, raw score) with conversational, emotionally resonant language (freaking out, sweating, clutch, nightmare).
Formatting Style Highly visual and scannable. Panicking students do not read walls of text. It relies on bolded key numbers, distinct headers, and flat, easily digestible bullet points.

Writing Style Rulebook
Writing Voice Rules
* Acknowledge the Stakes: Always start by validating why the reader is on the page. They are not here to learn math for fun; they are here because they need to know if they passed.
* Be the Anchor: Once the panic is acknowledged, switch to a calm, authoritative, and direct tone to deliver the formula or the calculator tool.
Sentence Construction Rules
* Front-Load the Solution: Give them the calculator or the basic formula immediately. Explain the deeper nuances (like weighted grades or GPA impacts) further down the page.
* Use Contrast: Follow up a complex explanation of grading curves or weighted averages with a blunt, one-sentence summary.
Do/Don't Style Guidelines
* DO use visceral, real-world academic scenarios (e.g., calculating what you need on the final to keep a B-minus).
* DO keep the math formatting brutally simple. Render formulas in bold plain text (e.g., Marks ÷ Total × 100 = Percentage).
* DON'T sound like a sterile textbook or a condescending teacher.
* DON'T use heavy formatting or complex equations for simple arithmetic.
Transition Patterns
* Use abrupt, conversational pivots to move from the emotional setup to the mathematical solution.
* Examples: "But let's look at the numbers.", "Here is the reality.", "The math is actually pretty simple."
Common Phrasing Patterns
* "We have all been there."
* "The problem with raw scores is..."
* "Think of it this way..."
SME / Pro Tip Requirement: "The Trench Truth"
When inserting advice, it must be a specific, strategic academic reality, not generic study advice.
The Trench Truth: Define a specific grading trap or academic reality, then provide the strategic solution. Example: "A 65% looks like a disaster on paper, but context is everything. If your professor grades on a strict bell curve and the class average was a 52%, that 65% is actually an A. Always check your syllabus to see if raw scores are the final metric before you panic."

Example Paragraph Rewrite
Original (Generic & Sterile): To calculate your percentage grade, you need to know how many marks you received and the total possible marks. First, divide the marks you got by the total marks available on the test. Next, multiply that decimal by 100. This will convert the number into a percentage, which will tell you your final grade for the assignment.
Rewritten (Applying the Extracted Tone): Staring at a raw score like 34/45 on the top of your midterm doesn't actually tell you much. Human brains do not naturally process weird fractions; we need to see a percentage to know if we are celebrating or stress-eating. The math to figure this out is straightforward, even if your brain feels fried. Just take your earned marks, divide them by the total possible marks, and multiply by 100. For this test, 34 divided by 45 is 0.755. Multiply that by 100, and you are sitting comfortably at 75.5%. Not too bad for a Tuesday.
